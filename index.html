Chapter One
The gap that drives everything

Before we get into wars and who starts them, you need to understand one thing about how money works at a global scale. It is simpler than it sounds.

All the paper "wealth" in the world
$300T
Stocks, bonds, mortgages, derivatives. Every financial claim that exists on paper.
Everything actually made and sold in a year
$110T
All the food, cars, houses, software, services. Everything real, produced in one year.

The financial world has nearly three times more claims than there are real things backing them up. That gap of roughly $195 trillion is not just an economics problem. It is the engine of modern war.

Here is why that matters
Money sitting still loses value. Capital must constantly expand, finding new real assets to absorb, new territories to enter, new markets to open. When it runs out of willing partners, it creates unwilling ones. Wars, sanctions, and regime changes are not failures of the system. They are the system working exactly as intended. Forced market openings for surplus capital.

This is not a fringe theory. It is a documented pattern that has played out with the same actors, the same methods, and the same results for over 70 years.

Chapter Two
How the system keeps itself alive

Once you understand the gap, the next question is obvious: how does the system keep finding new territories to absorb? The answer is a toolkit, used in roughly this order, from cheapest to most expensive:

1
Sanctions
Cut a country off from the global financial system. It cannot import medicine. It cannot export oil. The economy slowly suffocates. The country eventually opens up, or its government collapses. Currently active against Iran, Venezuela, Cuba, North Korea, Russia, and Myanmar at the same time.
2
IMF Debt
A country hits a financial crisis. The IMF offers a loan. The loan comes with conditions: privatize state companies, remove trade barriers, cut social spending. Foreign capital floods in. Local businesses cannot compete. The country becomes permanently dependent. Pakistan has entered IMF programs 24 times.
3
Regime Change
When sanctions are too slow, fund the opposition. Buy friendly media. Organize street protests. Install a more cooperative government. The CIA has done this in Iran (1953), Guatemala (1954), and Chile (1973). All three are now officially acknowledged by the US government.
4
War
The most expensive option, and the most profitable for certain industries. Weapons companies earn billions. Reconstruction firms win contracts. The defeated country's resources open to foreign capital. After Iraq: Halliburton received $39.5 billion in contracts. After Libya: oil fields reopened within months of Gaddafi's death.
"I spent 33 years in the Marines. I was a gangster for capitalism. I helped make Mexico safe for American oil interests. I helped make Haiti and Cuba a decent place for the National City Bank boys."
General Smedley Butler, most decorated US Marine in history, writing in 1935

He wrote that in 1935. The methods have been updated. The logic has not.

Chapter Three
The proof is in the pattern

Every conflict looks different on the surface. Different country. Different villain. Different reason given to the public. But underneath the official story, the same thing keeps showing up.

1951: Iran
Iran's elected leader nationalized the country's oil
He said Iranian oil should belong to Iranians. Two years later, a CIA-organized coup removed him. Oil was handed back to American and British companies. The CIA officially acknowledged this in 2013.
2000: Iraq
Saddam Hussein switched Iraq's oil sales from dollars to euros
Three years later, the US invaded. No weapons of mass destruction were found. Estimates of Iraqi deaths range from 150,000 documented civilian deaths to nearly half a million when including indirect casualties from destroyed healthcare and infrastructure. Oil sales returned to dollars. Halliburton and ExxonMobil entered. The leaked Downing Street Memo confirmed the intelligence was shaped around a decision already made.
2011: Libya
Gaddafi announced a gold-backed African currency for oil trade
This would have replaced the dollar in African oil transactions. Within weeks, NATO launched air strikes. Gaddafi was killed. Oil fields reopened within months. By 2017 there were open slave markets in Libya.
2022: Russia
Russia began selling gas in rubles and yuan instead of dollars
Russia was cut from SWIFT, the global payment backbone. $300 billion in Russian reserves held in Western banks were frozen. The stated reason was the Ukraine invasion. The financial response specifically targeted the currency switch.
2026: Iran, right now
Iran had been pricing oil in non-dollar currencies since 2023
February 28, 2026: US and Israel launched coordinated strikes. Supreme Leader Khamenei was killed in the opening hours. Over 1,230 Iranians have died. The pattern completes itself again.

The same countries, attacked by the same powers, for the same underlying reason. They stopped using the dollar for oil. This is not coincidence. This is a documented 70-year pattern. The official justifications change every time. The financial motive does not.

Interlude
Three thinkers who saw this coming

This framework did not come from nowhere. Historians, geographers, and economists have been mapping it for over a century. Here are three ideas worth sitting with.

David Harvey, Geographer at Oxford
"The Spatial Fix"

Harvey's core idea: when capital accumulates faster than it can be profitably reinvested at home, it must find new territory. Not because capitalists are evil, but because the system structurally requires it, or it collapses. He calls this the "spatial fix." The territories targeted are not random. They are places with resources, infrastructure, or markets that capital needs to absorb its surplus.

His question: what happens when there are no more new territories left to open? His answer: the system starts cannibalizing what already exists. He called this "accumulation by dispossession": taking what was once public or communal and making it private. Privatization of water. Enclosure of forests. Financialization of housing. It is the same logic turned inward.

"Finance capital backed by state power amounts to accumulation by other means."
David Harvey, The New Imperialism, 2003
Shashi Tharoor, Indian Parliamentarian and Historian
The Questions He Asked at Oxford

In 2015, Tharoor stood at the Oxford Union and asked a simple question: Britain came to India when India held 23% of the world's entire economic output. Britain left in 1947 when India held less than 4%. What happened to the other 19%?

His argument was that British industrialization was not a product of British genius. It was financed by systematic deindustrialization of India, destroying India's textile industry, redirecting Indian tax revenue to fund British wars around the world, and siphoning raw materials out while selling finished goods back at a markup. The same logic, running for 200 years, on a continental scale.

The reason this matters now: that logic did not disappear in 1947. It changed its clothes. The East India Company became the IMF. The Governor-General became the World Bank. The drain theory became structural adjustment. Tharoor's question about who benefits from the arrangement and at whose expense remains the right question to ask about every economic relationship today.

"It's a bit rich to oppress, enslave, kill and torture people for 200 years and then celebrate the fact that they are democratic at the end of it."
Shashi Tharoor, Oxford Union debate, May 2015
Lenin, Writing in 1916
Capitalism Needs an Outside

Before the First World War, Lenin studied the relationship between capital and empire and reached one conclusion: capitalism cannot sustain itself within its own borders. It must constantly find new markets, new labor, new resources, new territories. When domestic profit rates fall, capital exports itself. When capital exports itself, it needs political and military protection. When it needs protection, empires are built. He published this in 1916, while the war was still happening, as his explanation of why it was happening.

The reason this is worth knowing is not ideology. It is that the prediction has aged well. Every major geopolitical conflict since 1916 fits his framework better than it fits the official explanations given at the time. You do not have to agree with his politics to take the pattern seriously.

"The war was an annexationist, predatory, plunderous war. Its historical and economic background must be studied to understand modern politics."
Vladimir Lenin, Imperialism: The Highest Stage of Capitalism, 1916

Three different people, three different centuries, the same underlying observation: capital does not stay within borders. It expands, or it collapses. That expansion, when it runs out of willing partners, creates unwilling ones. Wars, sanctions, debt traps, and regime changes are all versions of the same process. Harvey named it. Tharoor documented it. Lenin predicted it.

The Downing Street Memo is real and freely available online. The PNAC document is real and online. The CIA's formal acknowledgment of the Iran coup is online. The most important evidence is not hidden. It just does not make the front page.
